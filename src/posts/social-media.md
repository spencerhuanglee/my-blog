# Social Media: A Power that Fractures
Ever since the Internet (Net) had existed, the promise had been that we would gain the power to more easily control and direct our lives through the power of accessible information, instant communication, and personalization. Though the means of these promises are real, the power of control we have on the direction of our life is quickly disappearing under our feet without us knowing.

# Hyper-Personalization
Companies such as Meta (Facebook & Instagram), Google, Microsoft, Apple, and Open AI give us the power to personalize how we navigate the Net through filters. Filters not in the sense of searching (though that is a tool of filtering), but as a toolkit that allows us to choose what we consume, such as books, music, videos, and people. In it's infant stages, this toolkit required us to manually configure what to consume based on our interest. Even in it's early stages, the consequences for this new potential of hyper-personalization could be catastrophic; hyper-personalization has the power to fracture our society, which was once a society that shared information and culture — now it is a society of diverging dialectics and fractured information.
### Introduction of the Algorithm
When the algorithm is introduced, personalization was no longer just an choice — it was a requirement. However, this requirement was not *just* personalization forced upon us — it was hyper-personalized *chosen* by these companies, locking us from how we wanted to navigate the Net, taking control away from us. Social media companies and tech companies have specialists dedicated to creating algorithms that know exactly what we want to consume. They first gave us the power to follow who we want to follow and see what we want to see — then they take it away.

One may ask, isn't it good that they know what I want and they can give it to me more easily and more accessibly? The answer is a definite **No**, take a look at how polarized our social media experiences are depending on the person. On all social media platforms, the left and the right have feeds filled with diametrically opposed opinions, but also *information* — more particularly, *disinformation*. In college, people form into groups with people who are like them in terms of majors, year, sports, interests, culture, and specific internet-driven social archetypes; groups and students are in close proximity and are exposed to other groups on a daily basis, independent of our control. This is not the case with social media, in which all experience and information is divided in their own digital tribes or subcultures that acts as an echo chamber, polarizing other subcultures. 

# Control
As humans, we aim to control anything that affects our lives, but these companies have given us no other option, to either be controlled or to be forgotten by our friends and family. Instagram, Twitter, and YouTube used to only show posts of people you follow or subscribe to, when you have seen all of the posts, that was the end of it. But with the advent of TikTok, the landscape changed — Instagram, Youtube, and Twitter adopted the short-form content (Reels, Shorts, and Immersive Media Viewer, respectively).

The algorithms paired with these short-form content capitalized on our attention spans along with our interests. Browsing any social media platform now feature short-form content on our feeds that were once controlled and curated by us. By its addictive design, we accept it so that we can still be active in a world dominated by online social interactions. But the problem runs deeper, not only are companies now able to curate our interests and people for us, they can now control our interests and people we interact with, with the sole goal of profit.

Think about the power they hold, they may present us content that could be misinformation or disinformation in order to swing us to one political side or the other with a simple injection of a video or tweet. They are not bound by morality but by profits and *only* profits. Advertisements may pay them to manipulate our opinions or thoughts. In the age of AI, information and data points can be assimilated to information that can describe nearly everything about us — guess who owns it, not us, them.

# How I take back Control
I have struggled with gaining control back to my own life in the way I see fit, and I still struggle. But I hope to give some insight on how I have made this struggle for control work in my favor. Some apps I do not own, such as Twitter and TikTok, but I will include alternatives based on the works of many great developers that are often open sourced and free. However, with one very **big** limitation — I am using android. Most of my friends and the general population use Apple devices, which does not allow sideloading or app modification; this philosophy Apple has taken takes control away from us even more. Of course, do your own research and there may be alternatives you can find for the Apple ecosystem. Reddit is your best friend when it comes to finding things to give control back to you.

## Instagram
Instagram is incredibly addicting with reels, but it is useful for connecting with friends in college and as a messaging platform. I have my Feed, Reels, and Explore section disabled. However, you can choose to still have feed enabled but disable suggested Reels or Posts in your feed. 

**For Android:** Download [InstaPrime](https://instaprime.net/), click on settings -> Focus Mode and choose which you want to disable. To keep Stories and Feeds, go to settings -> Feed and Stories and choose your desired options.

**For iPhone:** There is no dedicated Instagram app that can disable the addicting features. However, people have used [Beeper](https://help.beeper.com/en_US/chat-networks/instagram) to use retain Instagram's messaging features but without the addicting UI Instagram has designed.

## YouTube
YouTube is a great way to find and learn information about topics you are interested in, either for school or daily life. I have designed my YouTube such that I cannot be recommended anything so that it is an intentional tool I can use — instead of it using me. When I open YouTube, it brings me to my subscriptions page, where I can see the videos of creators which I am subscribed to. My subscriptions are intentional, with creators that make content I *genuinely* subscribe to. I have disabled all navigation tabs except for subscriptions and my profile to see history and liked videos. Thumbnails are still captures so I am not visually persuaded to watch the video.

YouTube allows you to disable the Shorts feed entirely. (Im not sure if this is accurate or still current)
- Open the YouTube app and tap your **Profile icon** (bottom right).
- Tap the **Settings gear** icon (top right).
- Select **Time management**.
- Locate the **Shorts feed limit** section and set it to **0 minutes**.
